Movie Review for the Tale of Princess Kaguya

Introduction

The Tale of Princess of Kaguya is a 2013 Studio Ghibli film directed by Isao Takahata. It is based on the 10th century Japanese literary tale, the Tale of the Bamboo Cutter. The story has multiple major themes, including coming of age, feminism/autonomy, aristocracy, as well as freedom and responsibility. While the film is animated, which makes the film seem like it is geared towards younger audiences, there is an allure to the film that appeals to everyone. This may be due to the universality of the themes as well as the small details that may be more noticed and appreciated by older audiences, such as the subtle changes in art style and music that reflects the emotion of the scenes.

Out of all the themes in the film, the one that stood out the most to me was the opposing themes of freedom and responsibility, possibly because it is one of the most relevant to my current life, as well as to many around me. Thinking about this theme, I realize that while the distinct artistic visuals of the film are especially appealing, the connections one can make to their own lives also contributes a lot to the attractiveness of this film.

Plot Synopsis

The story begins with a bamboo cutter from a mountain village who finds a small sprite inside a stick of bamboo. The sprite becomes a human baby who the bamboo cutter and his wife raise as their daughter. The baby grows remarkably fast, reinforcing the idea that the baby from the bamboo is a miracle or comes from divinity. The bamboo cutter is determined that the child is destined to be royalty. The child, who is later given the name Kaguya, is raised in the village and befriends the other village children as they always play and explore together. She is especially close to and eventually falls in love with Sutemaru, the elder brother figure among the group of village children.

Kaguya stays in the village until the bamboo cutter finds gold and luxurious fabrics in the bamboo grove. He takes it as a sign that Kaguya should be raised as royalty instead of a normal village girl , so he uses the fabric to make extravagant colorful clothing and the gold to move into the capital and build a palace for Kaguya. He also hires an etiquette tutor and servants for her, allowing her to live a complete aristocratic life. Despite the access to luxury and her natural talent for aristocratic activities, like playing instruments, Kaguya finds little enjoyment with this lifestyle. She continuously longs for her previous carefree village life.

Rumors of her talent and beauty attracted multiple high ranking suitors, including the emperor himself. When suitors came to her, she was not willing to marry anyone so she gave them impossible requests as conditions for marriage. When the emperor went to see Kaguya, the emperor tried to kidnap Kaguya. In that moment, she discovered her ability to become invisible upon will. At night, she prays to the moon to save her from this life. It is revealed that she was originally from the moon and came to earth seeking to experience human life. Once she realizes the moon people will come to take her away, she tells her family. Her father, the bamboo cutter, was willing to do anything to protect her and keep her on earth. He built a wall and called for guards, however it was all futile against the celestial beings who easily made all the mortals unconscious. Kaguya eventually was forced back to the moon and her memories were erased.

The Moon

One of the most impactful scenes to me was the ending of the film when it seemed like everything was torn away forcibly from Kaguya and her family on earth. It is like awakening from a vivid dream to a bleak reality (or having to leave a warm soft bed for an 8 am class on a cold rainy dark morningâ€¦). The moon people show up as all powerful figures at the end who are able to suppress any sort of mortal efforts that go against their will. They are illustrated as buddhist figures, one of the most common god-figures in Asian myths and legends. The visual design of the moon people reinforces their role in the story. They are floating on clouds as they enter and are colored with pale colors, making them look more statuesque.

These aspects emphasize their otherworldly-ness, as well as their role as the hand of destiny in this story. The very perfect visualization of them illustrates the moon as a happy place to be, yet Kaguya's sad expression right before her memory is erased tells the story that the moon is more of a prison than a paradise. This is in line with many mythological interpretations of the moon around the world, as there are many stories about people or animals being trapped on the moon. The moon in The Tale of Princess Kaguya plays a special role in the story, as it is both her escape from her suffering on earth and a place of restriction.

Being Free

The film also pictures the colorfulness and potential of freedom in human life very effectively. These moments in the film also stand out to me as they contrast a lot with the more serious scenes of Kaguya in the palace. The use of music, more saturated colors, and motion of the characters in the scene when Kaguya reunites with Sutemaru is a prime example:

In this scene, Kaguya is finally able to reunite with Sutemaru after being trapped in her aristocratic life for so long. Out of excitement and happiness, they run and fly together. The point of view of the film moves with Kaguya and Sutemaru as they fly, immersing the viewer into the surrounding scenery. Much like other scenes depicting nature in the film, vibrant colors are used in the background opposed to the more watered down colors of the backgrounds and vibrant colors only used for the colors elsewhere. This brings forward the beauty found in nature and emphasizes it as something that Kaguya loves about earth.

Even in her aristocratic life, she takes advantage of opportunities to reconnect with the natural world. For example, gardening in her mother's garden and going to see cherry blossoms.

These moments represent Kaguya's ability to find enjoyment in a life full of restricting tasks and expectations. Similar to the flying scene, the cherry blossom scene features more dynamic movement of the point of view and vibrant colors. However, it focuses more on Kaguya and her enjoyment of the moment rather than the entire surroundings.

At the end of the scene, a little kid bumps into Kaguya. While Kaguya does not think much of it, the child's mother immediately apologizes profusely to Kaguya, seeing that Kaguya was of higher social status. This action brings Kaguya back to the reality of her restricting life. It is difficult to simply interact with others due to the differences in status and wealth. The mood immediately dampens because of this and she leaves with her mother and lady in waiting.

Being Caged

Among the more serious moments of the film, one of the most impactful is during Kaguya's coming of age party. The celebration is meant to be for her yet she has to stay in the background and watch all the invited aristocrats party from the dark. The realization that everything was just for status and it had nothing to do with genuine celebration led into a scene where Kaguya runs away from the palace.

The rougher lines become very distinct in this scene as they show darker emotions and desperation to escape. The increased roughness in the art and animation style emphasizes the rawness of the emotions felt in the scene. The scene also has darker colors and increased shading compared to the usual lighter colors and gradually becomes fully grayscale as Kaguya tosses away her colorful robes. This illustrates the desperation to be free from the expectations of her new lifestyle and return to her previous simple life. It also foreshadows her return to the moon, as she is seen running towards the moon which is seen hanging low in the sky.

Another part of Kaguya's coming of age that was especially memorable is when she has to pluck her brows and paint her teeth black:

It is a part of her responsibility as an aristocratic girl to follow the aristocratic beauty standards even if it seemed unnatural to her. She gains a more powerful social status but actually loses power and autonomy over herself. At the end of the scene, she receives many gifts, including a bird in a cage. Seeing the bird, she unlocks the cage and allows it to fly away. This can be representative of her own desire to become free from her current life.

Relevance

Freedom and responsibility are contrasted in the film through various methods but there are some places where the line between the two becomes blurred and complicated. Kaguya still finds ways to enjoy her life while taking care of her new responsibilities. For example, when with her tutor, she finds opportunities to fool around and entertain herself, however she acts completely differently when in front of an audience. The absence of one also does not guarantee the presence of the other. When Kaguya escapes her restricting life on earth, she just returns to another restricting life on the moon. This reflects how freedom and responsibility can be to us in real life.

There are times that responsibilities, such as studying and work feel like they are restricting your freedom to do things, especially if there is extra pressure added from parents or just the job market. On the flip side, taking advantage of your freedom and realizing that technically no one is stopping you comes with the guilt of not being productive and not working towards your responsibilities. However, there are times that the two concepts are not polar opposites. Like in college, you have some freedom over what you want you are responsible for learning. Likewise, if you take the freedom to go out and explore, you will have to take responsibility for yourself and your own safety.

Depicted in the film, there is also a linear decrease in freedom, or even just the enjoyment of life in general as Kaguya becomes older. Being a celestial being, Kaguya grew up very fast, but her life events still followed that of a typical human on earth. The enjoyment of exploration in childhood gradually disappears as more responsibilities become present, making a simple care-free life out of reach.

It can be easy to fall into a dark hole thinking about how life may never be the same as when we did not know as much and did not have as many expectations to fulfill. This may partially contribute to the impactfulness of the film, as it reminds many that simplicity can be difficult to obtain. However, it is untrue that life will be miserable with more responsibilities. There is joy in simplicity, but there can also be joy that comes with understanding complexities and having autonomy over your life.(Also the thought of never being given enough responsibilities to make enough money can be depressing).

Final Remarks

Overall, I would recommend this film. The unique elements of the animation and art style, as well as the soundtrack immerse viewers into the film effectively. Additionally, it can leave viewers pondering about their own lives as they finish watching. 10/10, can be depressing if you think too much, but very worth watching.
